Curiosity is not a flaw

Curiosity = Interdisciplinarity

Post Date: 03 January,2026

I wasn’t sure what I wanted to become and always thought of it as a character flaw. Everything excites me. Anything that has to do with how humans engage with the environment, and sometimes even how we use it, fascinates me. For this reason, I never quite knew what to do, but I eventually realised it was never a flaw. It was a sign that I am meant to be a data scientist. Working with data allows you to be everywhere and nowhere at the same time, if we are being honest. In my degree, we do a lot, from mapping to modelling to decision-making, but the days I felt the most passion were the days I programmed in Python, managed and created a database for the first time, and cleaned and simulated data in Excel. Those were the moments where I was truly creating, which is ironic considering I have never been good at the creative arts, and even worse at drawing. I know I am meant to be a data scientist because even when the work got hard, and trust me, it does, I was still happy. Happily stressed. Because I was doing what I love: being everywhere all at once.

As I've come to understand my own curiostiy and indecisiveness, I realised my interests were never scattered, but were connected.

Study the science of art; study the art of science. Learn how to see. Realize that everything connects to everything else.

-Leonardo da Vinci,from 'The Notebooks of Leonardo da Vinci

Have I ever made a real decision?

Free will

Post Date: 05 January, 2026

Theories about free will have ex>isted for ages and have broken the minds of some of the greatest thinkers to ever grace this planet. When I speak about being “meant” to be a data scientist, I don’t mean it in a rigid or mystical sense. I mean it emotionally. I dream of being one, sometimes literally, and I genuinely love the work that comes with it. Still, I find myself wondering whether this was truly my fate, and whether any of us really have a choice at all. The idea that I may never have made an actual decision is, to be honest, uncomfortable. But at the same time, it makes a strange kind of sense. My love for working with data isn’t something I slowly developed over time. It was there almost immediately. And I think this is true for many of us. We often can’t fully explain why we love certain things or feel drawn to specific paths. We just do. This question reminded me of a video I recently watched that explores free will, determinism, and whether our choices are truly our own. It raises the possibility that what we experience as “choice” may simply be the most probable outcome based on everything that came before. That idea made me wonder whether we have ever made a real decision, or whether there is, metaphorically speaking, some supercomputer that determined that the most likely thing for me to love would be data science, based on patterns in my behaviour and experiences. The thought is both fascinating and unsettling. It would be incredible, and at the same time terrifying, if our lives were the result of something like a deeply trained model running quietly in the background. I could be very far off, especially given my limited understanding of the human brain, or any brain for that matter. Perhaps we have complete free will. Or perhaps we have some degree of it, shaped and constrained by a predetermined structure. Either way, it suddenly makes sense why this question has the power to break our brains.

Are we actually resistant to change?

Post Date: 12 March, 2026

We are often told that people do not change, or that people are naturally resistant to change, but how true is that? When we really think about it, is it change itself that people resist, or the idea of relinquishing control? Or is change only seen as negative when its impacts are uncertain and potentially harmful? Because the reality is, we change all the time, and we are constantly in pursuit of change. Marriage, travelling, new hobbies, a new job, a new degree, hiring more employees. All of these are forms of change that we actively seek out. So it seems that resistance to change is conditional. Change is often accepted when it feels certain and is expected to bring about something positive. But when change is uncertain, potentially harmful, or requires too much effort, we tend to avoid it, or at least try to control how we move through it. For example, a company might try cutting overtime before retrenching employees, or if retrenchment becomes unavoidable, they may proceed and then try to manage the consequences. Both options rely heavily on adaptability. While the latter may be more difficult to navigate, both are forms of negative change. In both situations, morale is affected, dynamics shift, productivity dips, and the long-term success of the company can change. Effectively adapting to these kinds of challenges, and even finding ways to turn them into opportunities, is what human geographers refer to as “resilience building.” It is about systems responding to risks by adjusting their behaviour in order to function in new environments. Even one of the most well-known ideas in evolution suggests that those who are more adaptable are more likely to survive and thrive. Maybe adaptability is something we all have, and something we can nurture more intentionally.

I’m not trying to oversimplify this. Innovation is hard – if it weren’t, everyone would do it. Change is hard – if it weren’t everyone would adapt easily. But if you’re finding that people are resisting the change that you’re proposing, that’s a very strong sign that you don’t understand what they need, and you haven’t articulated a clear vision of the future. Deep in their hearts, people love change. That’s why we actively seek it out when we want to make our lives better. If people are resisting your change, you’re not meeting their needs.

- Tim Kistelle (2012)
